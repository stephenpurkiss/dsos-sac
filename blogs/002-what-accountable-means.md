# What "Accountable" Actually Means

*Kofi Mensah, Governance Lead, March 2024*

When Rhys first described the SAC framework to me, I asked a governance question: accountable to whom?

This matters. "Accountability" without a specified audience is not accountability. It is a performance of accountability.

In the public sector, where I have spent most of my career, accountability runs to citizens, to Parliament, to audit bodies. The direction of the accountability relationship is specified. The mechanism for exercising it is defined.

In professional services, accountability traditionally runs to the regulator. The client is the recipient of the service, not the principal of the accountability relationship.

SAC changes the direction. Accountability runs to the client.

In practice, this means:
- Members commit to specific standards that their clients can verify
- Clients can report to SAC if those standards are not being met
- SAC investigates and makes a public finding

The finding is the mechanism. A member who is found not to have met their commitments is named. Not sanctioned by a regulator - named by an accountability body that they chose to join.

This sounds mild. In practice, for professional services firms where reputation matters, it is not mild at all.

The governance model for SAC is itself accountable. We use a modified commons governance structure: no single stakeholder class controls the body. Individual members, practitioner members, and institutional members all have representation. Decisions that affect the framework require consensus across stakeholder classes.

It is slower than top-down governance. It is more durable.
